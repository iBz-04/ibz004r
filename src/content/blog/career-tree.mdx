## Too Many Choices

Job titles are out of control now, you open a job board and it throws and fifty things at your face.

Most of those names dont mean much on their own and its hard to see how they connect or where you fit

You know, the human brain isn't built to compare numerous options, that's why young people are confused about what to do.

I built this tree as a starting point to give you a direction with concrete options to explore.

---
Since the beginning of science, careers have popped up based on demand, earlier days required more research and discovery within the basic sciences.
Today there are many layers that sit on top of the basic sciences, and those layers create new paths, to find teh perfect path for you its advised to be honest with yourself, consider your basic strengths, cognitive ability and geographic location.
For students and new grads, Its important to see your initial career as a portfolio, and not necessarily what you will be doing in the next 10 years, It is of my personal opinion to have 3 fields in mind, two directly connected to your passion and one that is a safety net.

## How To Find Your Branch

Most career advice is fluff, so do this instead and pick the work, pick the place, then test it in real life.

Ask this: what do you want to do on a random Tuesday at 9am.

### Start with the shape of the work

Ignore titles and look at the loop, look at what you do all day, what you ship, how often you get feedback, and how much control you have.

Here is the short version.

- If you like first principles, models, and asking why things work at all, stay closer to science, and no that does not mean you have to be a nature person because a lot of it is math, models, labs, theory, and slow stubborn thinking.
- If you like building real things, go toward engineering and hardware, that is materials, devices, circuits, and all the stuff that has to obey physics in public.
- If you like logic, systems, and making clean structures out of chaos, go toward software, you are building systems in code and then dealing with the mess humans make around them.
- If you like patterns, prediction, and squeezing signal out of messy data, go toward data and machine learning, yes this is where a lot of AI work lives now.
- If you want software to hit the real world and stop being just boxes on a screen, go toward robotics and autonomy, this is where things start moving and also start breaking.

### Your degree does not trap you

People act like your degree is supposed to decide your whole life, and I think that screws a lot of people up.

A degree usually tells you more about how you think than what job title you must have.

That said, if you did not start with the same advantages, the right degree can help a lot. If you went to a non-target school or do not already have a network, a good program or a school people know can open doors for you.

That way of thinking moves around more than people admit, so you do not need to do the obvious job that sits next to your degree name.

You can move from physics into software, from biology into data, from mechanical engineering into robotics, or from philosophy into product or policy, as long as you can actually do the work.

The degree matters less than people think, and the way your mind learned to attack problems matters more.

Take one problem like traffic in a city.

A physics person might begin with flow, bottlenecks, and feedback loops, then look for the point where the whole thing starts to jam.

A math person is more likely to turn it into a constrained optimization problem and ask what exactly we are trying to minimize, total time, fuel, waiting, cost.

A biology person might see something more like an ecosystem, lots of local behavior, adaptation, competition, cooperation, and small choices compounding into large patterns.

An engineer might say all of that is nice but what can we actually build, deploy, maintain, and not have break in six months.

A computer science person might ask what can be measured, what can be automated, and what kind of system can respond in real time without falling over.

That is what I mean by a degree shaping how you think, it gives you a first instinct and kind of a default lens, but it is not a prison cell.

### Use interests as a compass

If you like the topic, learning hurts less, which is nice, but it is not enough.

Use the O*NET Interest Profiler if you want a simple way to get options, then pick two branches and test them.

### Use personality to predict friction

Personality will not pick a career for you, but it does show friction, it tells you what will get old fast and what will start annoying you by Tuesday.

If you hate uncertainty, avoid constant chaos, and if you hate routine, avoid pure maintenance.

### Don’t ignore values and environment

You can pick the right branch and still hate your life in the wrong place, so if a place makes you feel small or fake all day, leave even if the role looks perfect on paper.

### Where you work changes the job

Where you work changes the job way more than people admit.

- **Big companies**
  
  Stable, clear, and a little bit allergic to moving fast, good if you like predictability, annoying if you need speed.

- **Big Tech**
  
  Huge scale, strong systems, strong people, and enough process to make you question a few things, great if you like reliability and performance work, bad if you want full ownership right away.

- **Frontier labs**
  
  High stakes, fast research, lots of ambiguity, and not much calm, great if you like deep work and uncertainty, bad if you want a normal week.

- **Mid size companies**
  
  Usually the least stupid balance, real customers, real ownership, less theater, and good if you want room to move without total chaos.

- **Small companies**
  
  More ownership, faster feedback, and usually less drama than a startup, you still wear many hats, just without the full circus.

- **Startups**
  
  Everything changes fast and half the plan is fake by next month, great if you want responsibility early and you can handle uncertainty, rough if you need steady plans.

### “Born to do” is something you build

You build this over time, you get good at something, people trust you, you get more control, and the work starts to feel like yours instead of just being a job you fell into.

Dont wait around for passion to arrive fully formed, build it, then see if it holds up when it gets hard.

### Reality check: pick a branch with tailwinds

The world changes, so your plan has to change too.

AI and data skills matter, security matters too, and green work and healthcare keep growing, so learn skills that transfer and do not lock yourself into one narrow tool.

### Validate your branch with small experiments

You do not have to commit before you learn, and the fastest way to reduce career uncertainty is to run small, cheap tests.

Two ideas matter here, you can shape a job, and markets move.

You shape a job by taking on better problems, asking for different responsibilities, and making more room to think.

Make the test look like the job, do a small project, do a short internship, and do something where you actually feel the work a little bit.

One extra signal is flow, notice what pulls you in.
